## Festivals of Assam

### Bihu Festival of Assam
Bihu is the most important and widely celebrated festival of Assam. It represents Assamese culture, agriculture, and community life. Bihu is closely connected to the farming cycle and seasons.

**Types of Bihu:**

1. **Rongali Bihu (Bohag Bihu)**
   - Celebrated in April (mid-April, around 14th-15th)
   - Marks the Assamese New Year (1st day of Bohag month)
   - Celebrates the beginning of the agricultural season
   - Duration: 7 days of celebration
   - Activities: Bihu dance, traditional songs, community gatherings
   - Traditional clothes: Mekhela Chador (women), Dhoti-Kurta with Gamosa (men)
   - Special rituals: Goru Bihu (cattle worship), Manuh Bihu (human celebration)
   - Traditional games: Buffalo fighting, egg fighting, pot breaking

2. **Bhogali Bihu (Magh Bihu)**
   - Celebrated in January (mid-January, around 14th-15th)
   - Known as the festival of food and harvest celebration
   - Marks the end of the harvest season
   - Duration: 2-3 days
   - Activities: Community feasts, traditional cooking competitions
   - Special structures: Meji (temporary bamboo and straw huts), Bhelaghar (community houses)
   - Traditional foods: Pitha, Laru, Til (sesame) preparations
   - Morning ritual: Meji burning at dawn with prayers

3. **Kongali Bihu (Kati Bihu)**
   - Celebrated in October (mid-October)
   - A more spiritual and quiet festival
   - Time of scarcity (Kongali means poor/scarce)
   - Activities: Lighting earthen lamps (Akash Banti) in paddy fields
   - Prayers offered for good crops and prosperity
   - Traditional foods: Simple vegetarian meals
   - Cultural significance: Remembering ancestors, spiritual reflection

### Ambubachi Mela
- **Location**: Kamakhya Temple, Nilachal Hill, Guwahati
- **Duration**: 4 days in June (during monsoon)
- **Significance**: Annual menstruation period of Goddess Kamakhya
- **Attendees**: Over 1 million devotees, sadhus, tantrics
- **Rituals**: Temple remains closed for 3 days, reopens on 4th day
- **Cultural importance**: Celebrates feminine power and fertility
- **Economic impact**: Major boost to local tourism and economy

### Jonbeel Mela
- **Location**: Dayang Belguri, Morigaon district
- **Duration**: 3 days in January
- **Unique feature**: Ancient barter system (no money exchange)
- **Communities**: Tiwa, Karbi, Khasi tribes and plain dwellers
- **Items traded**: Agricultural products, handmade crafts, traditional items
- **Cultural significance**: Promotes inter-community harmony
- **Historical importance**: Continues 500-year-old tradition

### Ali-Aye-Ligang
- **Community**: Mising tribe
- **Time**: February (beginning of spring)
- **Significance**: Marks start of sowing season
- **Duration**: 5 days
- **Rituals**: Worship of Donyi-Polo (Sun-Moon), traditional dances
- **Traditional foods**: Apong (rice beer), Purang Apin (special rice cake)
- **Cultural activities**: Gumrag dance, traditional songs

### Majuli Festival
- **Location**: Majuli Island (world's largest river island)
- **Duration**: 3 days in November
- **Highlights**: Sattriya dance, mask-making, pottery
- **Cultural focus**: Neo-Vaishnavite traditions
- **Attractions**: Satra visits, traditional boat races
- **Crafts showcase**: Handloom weaving, traditional masks

### Bwisagu (Bodo New Year)
- **Community**: Bodo people
- **Time**: April (same time as Rongali Bihu)
- **Duration**: 7 days
- **Traditional dance**: Bagurumba dance
- **Traditional dress**: Dokhona (women), Gamsa (men)
- **Special foods**: Ondla (rice cake), Narzi (local brew)

### Kherai Puja
- **Community**: Bodo people
- **Significance**: Worship of Bathou deity (supreme god)
- **Rituals**: Performed by Doudini (female priest)
- **Offerings**: Flowers, rice, local fruits
- **Cultural importance**: Preserves ancient Bodo religious traditions

### Doul Utsav (Assamese Holi)
- **Main location**: Barpeta (famous for celebrations)
- **Duration**: 2 days
- **Activities**: Devotional songs, traditional dances
- **Religious significance**: Celebrates Lord Krishna
- **Cultural fusion**: Combines Vaishnavite traditions with local customs

### Chavang Kut
- **Community**: Kuki tribe
- **Time**: November (post-harvest)
- **Significance**: Thanksgiving for successful harvest
- **Traditional dance**: Kuki folk dances
- **Community activities**: Feast sharing, cultural programs

## Traditional Assamese Food Culture

### Staple Foods
- **Rice**: Primary food eaten 3 times daily
- **Fish**: Freshwater fish from rivers and ponds
- **Vegetables**: Seasonal local vegetables, bamboo shoots
- **Dal**: Various lentils, especially masoor and moong
- **Tea**: Assam tea consumed multiple times daily

### Traditional Cooking Methods
- **Bhap**: Steaming (healthiest method)
- **Jolokia**: Using ghost pepper for spice
- **Khar**: Alkaline cooking using banana ash
- **Tenga**: Sour preparations using tomatoes, lemon

### Festival Foods

**During Bihu:**
- **Pitha varieties**: Til Pitha, Ghila Pitha, Sunga Pitha, Tekeli Pitha
- **Laru types**: Til Laru (sesame), Narikol Laru (coconut)
- **Jolpan items**: Chira (flattened rice), Muri (puffed rice), Akhoi
- **Special preparations**: Payesh (rice pudding), Kheer

**Daily Traditional Dishes:**
- **Masor Tenga**: Sour fish curry with tomatoes or lemon
- **Khar**: Alkaline dish with raw papaya, bottle gourd
- **Aloo Pitika**: Mashed potato with mustard oil, onions, green chilies
- **Bhaji**: Various vegetable preparations
- **Xaak**: Leafy green vegetables (paleng, dhekia, matikaduri)

### Assam Tea Culture
- **Varieties**: Black tea, Green tea, White tea
- **Famous gardens**: Jorhat, Dibrugarh, Tezpur regions
- **Preparation**: Strong tea with milk and sugar
- **Social importance**: Offered to guests, consumed during discussions
- **Economic significance**: Major export product, employment generator

## Arts, Crafts & Cultural Elements

### Traditional Textiles
- **Muga Silk**: Golden silk unique to Assam, UNESCO recognized
- **Eri Silk**: Peace silk, eco-friendly production
- **Pat Silk**: White silk used for religious occasions
- **Cotton weaving**: Traditional handloom cotton fabrics
- **Mekhela Chador**: Traditional Assamese women's attire
- **Gamosa**: Sacred cloth with cultural and religious significance

### Traditional Crafts
- **Bamboo crafts**: Baskets, furniture, decorative items
- **Cane work**: Traditional furniture and household items
- **Pottery**: Kumhar community traditional pottery
- **Mask making**: Majuli island specialty for cultural performances
- **Bell metal work**: Traditional utensils and decorative items
- **Wood carving**: Decorative items and furniture

### Music and Dance
- **Bihu dance**: Performed during Bihu festivals
- **Sattriya dance**: Classical dance form from Sattras
- **Bagurumba**: Traditional Bodo dance
- **Instruments**: Dhol, Pepa, Taal, Gogona, Xutuli
- **Folk songs**: Bihu geet, Tokari geet, Kamrupi lokgeet

- **Ceremonial wear**: Special designs for festivals and weddings

## Language & Communication

### Assamese Language Basics
- **Script**: Assamese script (derived from Brahmi)
- **Speakers**: Over 15 million people
- **Dialects**: Eastern, Central, Western Assamese

### Common Expressions & Greetings
- **"Namaskar"** = Hello/Goodbye (formal)
- **"Adaab"** = Respectful greeting
- **"Bhal lagise"** = Feels good/I like it
- **"Kene ase?"** = How are you?
- **"Ki kori asa?"** = What are you doing?
- **"Apunar name ki?"** = What is your name?
- **"Mur name hol..."** = My name is...
- **"Dhanyabad"** = Thank you
- **"Khyama koribi"** = Please forgive/Excuse me
- **"Bhaal thakibi"** = Stay well/Take care

### Numbers in Assamese
- **Ek** = One, **Dui** = Two, **Tini** = Three
- **Sari** = Four, **Pas** = Five, **Soy** = Six
- **Xaat** = Seven, **Aat** = Eight, **Noy** = Nine, **Doh** = Ten

### Family Relations
- **Maa** = Mother, **Deuta** = Father
- **Bhai** = Brother, **Bhoini** = Sister
- **Aita** = Grandmother, **Koka** = Grandfather

## Historical & Cultural Significance

### Ahom Dynasty (1228-1826)
- **Duration**: 600 years of rule
- **Contributions**: Administrative system, cultural development
- **Architecture**: Rang Ghar, Talatal Ghar, Kareng Ghar
- **Legacy**: Influenced modern Assamese culture and identity

### Srimanta Sankardev (1449-1568)
- **Contribution**: Neo-Vaishnavite movement founder
- **Cultural impact**: Sattriya dance, Borgeet songs, Ankiya Nat drama
- **Philosophy**: Eka Sarana Dharma (devotion to one God)
- **Legacy**: Sattras (monasteries) across Assam

### Tribal Communities
- **Bodo**: Largest tribal community, own autonomous council
- **Mising**: River island dwellers, skilled in fishing and farming
- **Karbi**: Hill tribe, known for traditional crafts
- **Tiwa**: Ancient community, traditional village councils
- **Rabha**: Known for traditional medicine and crafts

### Economy
- **Tea industry**: Major contributor to state economy
- **Oil & Gas**: Digboi (Asia's first oil refinery)
- **Agriculture**: Rice, jute, sugarcane production
- **Handloom**: Traditional textile industry

- **Assamese literature**: Rich tradition of poetry and prose
- **Universities**: Gauhati University, Dibrugarh University
- **Cultural institutions**: Sangeet Natak Akademi, Sahitya Sabha

### Challenges & Opportunities
- **Flood management**: Annual Brahmaputra flooding
- **Infrastructure development**: Connectivity improvements
- **Cultural preservation**: Maintaining traditions in modern times
- **Tourism potential**: Unexplored natural and cultural sites
